# INSTRUMENT 1 — PSS-10
## Perceived Stress Scale

**What this instrument measures:**
The PSS-10 measures how often, over the past month, life has felt uncontrollable,
unpredictable, or overwhelming. It captures the subjective experience of stress —
not the number of stressful events, but how much pressure a person has felt from
their circumstances. Two people can face identical situations and experience them
very differently. The PSS-10 measures that difference.

---

### PSS-LOW — Total score 0 to 13 (Low band)

In this range, stress is not experienced as overwhelming. Life feels broadly
manageable, and there is a general sense of being able to cope with what is being
asked. This does not mean life is without difficulty — it means the demands feel
proportionate to the capacity to meet them.

Research context: The average PSS-10 score in the general adult population is
around 15 to 16 (Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2012). A score in the low range places
this person below the population average, indicating a stress load that is lighter
than most people experience day to day. This is a meaningful resource — a sense
of manageability that not everyone has access to.

---

### PSS-MODERATE — Total score 14 to 26 (Moderate band)

In this range, stress is a real and present feature of life. There are moments
of feeling overloaded, of struggling to keep up, of sensing that circumstances
are not fully within control. This is the most common experience — the majority
of adults score in this range. Being here is not a sign that something is wrong.
It is a sign that life is making real demands.

Research context: Most adults in the general population score between 14 and 26
on the PSS-10. This range has been associated with some difficulty managing
competing demands and periodic feelings of loss of control, but also with
retained capacity to cope (Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2012). The specific shape
of the stress — whether it feels more like helplessness or more like low
confidence — carries different clinical meaning and is explored further below.

---

### PSS-HIGH — Total score 27 to 40 (High band)

In this range, stress has become something closer to a constant presence. Life
feels unpredictable, difficult to control, and at times overwhelming. The sense
that difficulties are piling up faster than they can be managed is familiar.
This is not a character flaw or a sign of weakness. It is a physiological and
psychological response to sustained pressure that has exceeded the system's
current capacity to absorb it.

Research context: Sustained scores in this range carry documented physiological
consequences. High perceived stress has been associated with elevated cortisol
levels, suppressed immune function, increased susceptibility to infection, slower
wound healing, and markers of accelerated biological aging (Cohen & Janicki-Deverts,
2012). The body and the mind do not separate stress from its physical toll.
This is not a warning designed to frighten — it is the research community's way
of saying that what this person is carrying is real, significant, and worth taking
seriously.

---

### PSS-HELPLESSNESS-HIGH — Helplessness subscale elevated

The helplessness dimension of stress captures how often life feels out of control —
how often circumstances feel impossible to influence, how often things feel like
they are happening to a person rather than being shaped by them. When this is
elevated, the dominant experience is one of being acted upon rather than acting.
This is one of the most exhausting forms of stress, because there is no clear
action that feels available.

Research context: Perceived helplessness is closely linked to the development
of learned helplessness — a state in which the nervous system stops expecting
that effort will produce change, and begins conserving energy by withdrawing from
attempts to influence outcomes (Seligman, 1975). Elevated helplessness on the
PSS-10 is associated with increased risk of depression, burnout, and chronic stress
sequelae when it persists over time (Cohen & Williamson, 1988).

---

### PSS-EFFICACY-LOW — Self-efficacy subscale low

The self-efficacy dimension captures how confident a person feels in their ability
to handle what life brings. When self-efficacy is low, even manageable demands
can feel threatening, because the sense that one can meet them is absent. This
is distinct from helplessness — it is less about life being out of control and
more about doubting one's own capacity to navigate it.

Research context: Self-efficacy is one of the most robust predictors of resilience
under stress. When self-efficacy is intact, people tolerate higher stress loads
without the same physiological consequences. When it collapses, even moderate
stress becomes more damaging (Bandura, 1997; Cohen & Williamson, 1988).
Rebuilding self-efficacy — through small concrete successes and accurate
self-assessment — is often the most direct route out of this pattern.

# INSTRUMENT 2 — PHQ-8
## Patient Health Questionnaire — Depression

**What this instrument measures:**
The PHQ-8 measures the presence and severity of depression symptoms over the
past two weeks. It covers eight core dimensions of depression: loss of pleasure,
low mood, sleep disturbance, fatigue, appetite changes, feelings of worthlessness,
concentration difficulties, and psychomotor changes. It was specifically designed
for use in settings where a clinician is not present to follow up on every
response — making it well suited to this kind of online assessment.

---

### PHQ-NONE — Total score 0 to 4 (None to minimal)

Depression symptoms in this range are not clinically significant. There may be
difficult days and moments of low mood — that is part of being human — but they
are not forming a pattern that meets the threshold for clinical concern.
The majority of people in the general population score in this range.

Research context: Approximately 76% of adults in general population samples score
below 5 on the PHQ-8 (Kroenke et al., 2009). A score in this range is consistent
with the absence of a depressive disorder. If low mood is present at all,
it is situational and responsive rather than persistent and pervasive.

---

### PHQ-MILD — Total score 5 to 9 (Mild)

Some depression symptoms are present. They may not feel overwhelming, but they
are there — a flatness, a difficulty finding pleasure in things that used to
feel meaningful, some tiredness that does not fully lift. This range is worth
paying attention to, not because it signals crisis, but because it signals
that something in the emotional system is under strain.

Research context: Approximately 18% of adults score in this range in general
population studies (Kroenke et al., 2009). Mild depressive symptoms are associated
with reduced daily functioning and lower quality of life even without meeting
criteria for a depressive disorder. They also represent a point at which
intervention can prevent progression to a more significant depressive episode.

---

### PHQ-MODERATE — Total score 10 to 14 (Moderate)

Depression is present and affecting daily life. The symptoms are consistent enough
to interfere with the ability to do what needs to be done — to concentrate,
to sustain interest, to feel adequate, to maintain energy and sleep. This is not
a light load. It is a clinical level of depression that warrants attention.

Research context: A score of 10 or above on the PHQ has a sensitivity of 81%
and a positive predictive value of 92% for major depression (Kroenke et al., 2001).
In plain terms: scoring here means there is a very high likelihood that a genuine
depressive disorder is present. This is not a screening false positive — it is
a meaningful clinical signal that professional support is indicated.

---

### PHQ-MODERATELY-SEVERE — Total score 15 to 19 (Moderately severe)

Depression at this level is substantially disrupting daily life. The symptoms
are not occasional or mild — they are consistent, pervasive, and affecting
the ability to function across most areas. Getting through the day requires
significant effort. Things that used to feel manageable may feel impossible.
This is a level of depression that warrants active and prompt clinical support.

Research context: Scores in this range are consistent with major depressive
disorder at a severity that substantially affects daily functioning and quality
of life (Kroenke et al., 2009). Research consistently shows that depression at
this level responds well to treatment — both psychological and, where appropriate,
pharmacological. The most important thing about scoring here is that it is not
a fixed state. It is a point in a trajectory that can change with the right support.

---

### PHQ-SEVERE — Total score 20 to 24 (Severe)

Depression at this level is profound and pervasive. The symptoms are present
at their most intense across nearly all dimensions — mood, energy, sleep,
concentration, sense of self-worth. This is depression that profoundly disrupts
daily life and requires immediate clinical attention.

Research context: Scores in this range are consistent with severe major depressive
disorder (Kroenke et al., 2009). Research indicates that severe depression of
this level typically requires combined treatment — psychological support alongside
medication review with a qualified prescriber. This is not a sign that things
cannot get better. It is a sign of how much the system is currently carrying,
and how much support it needs right now.

---

### PHQ-SOMATIC-HIGH — Somatic items elevated (items 3, 4, 5, 8)

The physical dimensions of depression — sleep, energy, appetite, and psychomotor
functioning — are particularly prominent in this profile. Depression does not
only live in thoughts and feelings. It lives in the body: in the tiredness that
sleep does not fix, in the appetite that shifts without reason, in the heaviness
that makes movement feel effortful. When the body is carrying the weight of
depression this visibly, it matters how we talk about it.

Research context: Depression with prominent somatic features is associated with
trauma history, somatisation, and nervous system dysregulation (Kirmayer, 2001).
It is also the form of depression most likely to be missed or misattributed —
people in this profile often do not identify as depressed because they experience
the body symptoms without the emotional vocabulary. Approaching this profile through
body experience rather than emotional language tends to be more effective.

---

### PHQ-COGNITIVE-HIGH — Cognitive and affective items elevated (items 1, 2, 6)

The inner experience of depression — loss of pleasure, persistent low mood,
and feelings of worthlessness or failure — is prominent in this profile.
These are the dimensions of depression that affect how a person sees themselves
and their world. The narrative of who they are, what they deserve, and what
is possible can become deeply affected when these items are elevated.

Research context: High scores on cognitive and affective depression items are
associated with dysthymic or longer-standing depressive presentations, and with
negative core beliefs about the self (Beck et al., 1979; Kroenke et al., 2009).
They are also the items most responsive to cognitive and meaning-making approaches
in therapy. The inner story is both the wound and the place where healing begins.

# INSTRUMENT 3 — MAIA-2
## Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness

**What this instrument measures:**
The MAIA-2 measures interoceptive awareness — the relationship between a person
and their body's internal signals. It covers eight dimensions of how people
notice, respond to, and use body sensations in daily life. This is not about
physical fitness or health. It is about whether the body is experienced as
a source of information, a source of threat, a place of safety, or something
largely absent from conscious experience. These patterns are deeply shaped
by history — especially by experiences that taught the body whether it was
safe to be present in.

No overall total score is calculated for the MAIA-2. Each subscale tells its
own story. The pattern across subscales is more clinically informative than
any single number.

---

## Not-Distracting subscale

**What it measures:**
Whether a person tends to stay present with sensations of physical discomfort
or pain, versus distracting away from them. High scores mean staying present.
Low scores mean moving away from body experience through distraction,
suppression, or avoidance.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile (score below 1.52)

There is a strong tendency to move away from uncomfortable body sensations
rather than staying with them. When the body signals discomfort, the default
response is to redirect attention elsewhere — to push through, to distract,
to occupy the mind with something else. This is a common and understandable
pattern, especially when body experience has historically been associated
with pain, threat, or overwhelm.

Research context: Low Not-Distracting scores are associated with avoidant
coping with somatic experience and are common in trauma presentations where
the body has become a source of threat rather than information (Mehling et al.,
2018). The pattern is not a character trait — it is a learned protective
response. The body learned to be avoided. It can learn to be approached again,
carefully and gradually.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile (score 1.52 to 2.60)

There is some capacity to stay present with body discomfort, though this is
not yet a consistent strength. Sometimes sensations can be noticed and stayed
with. At other times — particularly when the discomfort is more intense —
the impulse to distract or redirect is stronger. This middle ground is
a functional place to work from.

Research context: Most people in the general population fall in this range
(Mehling et al., 2018). The capacity to stay with body sensations is something
that develops with practice and with accumulated safety. Being in this range
suggests the foundation is present even if it is not yet robust.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-STRONG — Above 75th percentile (score above 2.60)

There is a meaningful capacity to remain present with physical discomfort
without automatically moving away from it. Sensations can be noticed and
tolerated without the immediate impulse to suppress or redirect. This is
a genuine clinical resource — one that many people do not have, and one
that forms the foundation for body-based therapeutic work.

Research context: Higher Not-Distracting scores are associated with more
adaptive interoceptive processing and greater capacity for somatic awareness
in therapeutic contexts (Mehling et al., 2018). This capacity predicts better
engagement with and response to body-based interventions.

---

## Not-Worrying subscale

**What it measures:**
Whether body sensations trigger anxiety, catastrophising, or emotional
escalation. High scores mean sensations can be noticed without spiralling.
Low scores mean body sensations tend to amplify into fear or worry.

### NOT-WORRYING-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile (score below 1.95)

Physical sensations tend to trigger anxiety and worry. When something is
noticed in the body — discomfort, pain, an unusual feeling — the mind
moves quickly toward concern, catastrophising, or emotional distress.
The body becomes a source of threat signals rather than neutral information.
This pattern is often exhausting because the body is never truly quiet.

Research context: Low Not-Worrying scores are specifically linked to anxiety
and to alexithymia — difficulty identifying and labelling emotional states
(Desdentado et al., 2022). Research shows that low scores on this subscale
predict depression and emotion dysregulation more strongly than most other
interoceptive measures. The body-anxiety feedback loop is real and documented:
body sensations trigger anxiety, anxiety amplifies body sensations, and the
cycle sustains itself (Mehling et al., 2018).

### NOT-WORRYING-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile (score 1.95 to 3.09)

Body sensations do not consistently trigger anxiety, though they sometimes
do — particularly when discomfort is more intense or when stress is already
high. There is some capacity to notice a sensation without immediately worrying
about what it means. This is a functional middle ground.

Research context: In this range, the relationship with body sensations is
neither predominantly anxious nor predominantly equanimous. Context matters:
under lower stress, sensations can be noticed calmly; under higher stress,
they are more likely to trigger concern (Mehling et al., 2018).

### NOT-WORRYING-STRONG — Above 75th percentile (score above 3.09)

Body sensations can generally be noticed without triggering anxiety or
worry. There is a capacity for equanimity with physical discomfort —
the ability to notice something uncomfortable and stay regulated while
doing so. This is a meaningful resource for somatic therapeutic work.

Research context: Higher Not-Worrying scores are associated with lower
anxiety and better emotion regulation (Desdentado et al., 2022; Mehling
et al., 2018). This subscale is one of the strongest interoceptive
predictors of emotional wellbeing.

---

## Attention Regulation subscale

**What it measures:**
The ability to deliberately direct and sustain attention toward body sensations.
High scores mean attention can be consciously brought to the body and held there.
Low scores mean this kind of intentional body focus is difficult to achieve
or maintain.

### ATTENTION-REG-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile (score below 2.26)

Deliberately directing attention to the body is difficult. Attempts to focus
on internal body experience tend to be brief, easily disrupted, or hard
to initiate. This is not about whether body sensations are present — it is
about whether conscious attention can be brought to them on purpose and
held there.

Research context: Low Attention Regulation scores are associated with
difficulty using body-based coping strategies and with cognitive-dominant
coping styles where thinking overtakes sensing (Mehling et al., 2018).
They do not indicate that body awareness is impossible — they indicate
that the attentional capacity to access it deliberately needs development.

### ATTENTION-REG-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile (score 2.26 to 3.42)

Some capacity to direct and sustain attention to the body exists, though
it varies. In quieter moments or with effort, attention can be brought
to body experience. In busier or more distressing moments, this becomes
harder to maintain. This is the typical range for most adults.

Research context: Most people in the general population fall in this range
(Mehling et al., 2018). Attention Regulation is a skill that is specifically
responsive to practices like mindfulness, body scan, and somatic awareness
exercises. Being in this range suggests the capacity is present and can
be developed.

### ATTENTION-REG-STRONG — Above 75th percentile (score above 3.42)

There is a strong ability to consciously direct and sustain attention toward
body sensations, even in the presence of external distractions or internal
mental activity. This is a genuine attentional resource — the ability to
choose to attend to the body and to stay there.

Research context: Higher Attention Regulation scores predict better engagement
with and response to mindfulness-based and somatic interventions (Mehling et al.,
2018). This capacity is associated with greater embodied self-awareness and
more flexible use of body experience in emotional processing.

---

## Emotional Awareness subscale

**What it measures:**
Awareness of the connection between body sensations and emotional states —
the felt sense of emotions in the body. High scores mean emotions are
experienced as embodied events. Low scores mean the body-emotion connection
is limited or absent — emotions may be known intellectually without being
felt physically.

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile (score below 2.79)

The connection between body sensations and emotional states is not well
established. Emotions may be understood as concepts — named, recognised,
discussed — without being fully experienced as body events. There may be
a quality of emotional experience that feels somewhat abstract or hard to
locate in the body. This is sometimes described as knowing what you feel
without feeling it.

Research context: Low Emotional Awareness scores are associated with
alexithymia — a difficulty identifying emotions through bodily signals —
which is particularly common in trauma survivors with chronic dissociation
(Mehling et al., 2018; Desdentado et al., 2022). The body-emotion bridge
is one that develops through safety, attention, and repeated experience
of emotions being survivable. When it is limited, it does not mean
emotional experience is absent — it means the somatic channel for
emotional information is not yet available.

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile (score 2.79 to 4.09)

There is some awareness of how emotions manifest in the body, though this
is not yet a consistent strength. At times, emotions can be felt as body
events — a tightening, a heaviness, a lightness. At other times, the
connection is less clear. This middle ground is workable territory.

Research context: Developing Emotional Awareness is associated with emerging
capacity for felt sense experience. This subscale is sensitive to therapeutic
work that focuses on the body-emotion connection — somatic approaches,
focusing-oriented therapy, and mindfulness practices that attend to
emotional body experience (Mehling et al., 2018).

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-STRONG — Above 75th percentile (score above 4.09)

There is strong awareness of how emotions are experienced in the body.
When something is felt emotionally, there is a corresponding body
experience that is noticed and available for reflection. This is the
felt sense capacity — the ability to know what is happening emotionally
by reading the body's signals. This is a significant resource for
emotional processing and self-understanding.

Research context: High Emotional Awareness is associated with better
emotion regulation, lower alexithymia, and greater capacity for
integrative emotional processing (Desdentado et al., 2022; Mehling
et al., 2018). It is one of the most important resources for
trauma-focused and somatic therapeutic work.

---

## Self-Regulation subscale

**What it measures:**
The ability to use body awareness to regulate distress — to find a sense
of calm or steadiness through conscious attention to the body and breath.
High scores mean the body is available as a regulatory resource.
Low scores mean this route to regulation is not currently accessible.

### SELF-REG-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile (score below 2.10)

The body is not currently available as a tool for managing distress.
When things become overwhelming, turning attention to the body or
breath does not produce a sense of calm — it may even increase distress.
The regulatory pathway through body awareness is not yet open.
This does not mean regulation is impossible. It means this particular
route is currently unavailable, and other regulation strategies are needed.

Research context: Low Self-Regulation scores are the single most clinically
significant finding in the MAIA-2 for treatment sequencing. Research
consistently shows that body-based regulatory capacity is a prerequisite
for safe trauma processing — without it, accessing trauma material
risks overwhelming the system without the ability to return to baseline
(Van der Kolk, 2014; Levine, 2010; Mehling et al., 2018).
Low Self-Regulation does not predict poor outcomes. It identifies
where the work needs to begin.

### SELF-REG-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile (score 2.10 to 3.46)

Some capacity to use body awareness for self-regulation exists. At times —
particularly in lower-stress moments — attending to the breath or bringing
awareness to the body produces a settling effect. At other times, this
is harder to access. The regulatory capacity is present but not yet
robust or reliably available under pressure.

Research context: This range indicates a developing regulatory resource —
one that exists and can be built on, but that is not yet a strong
and consistent tool (Mehling et al., 2018). This is workable clinical
territory. The foundation is there. Building on it — through paced
somatic practice and graduated exposure to body experience — is
the path forward.

### SELF-REG-STRONG — Above 75th percentile (score above 3.46)

Body awareness is a genuine and reliable regulatory resource. When
distress arises, attending to the breath, finding a point of steadiness
in the body, or bringing conscious attention to physical sensation
produces a real sense of settling. The body can be used to come back
to baseline. This is one of the most significant resources a person
can bring to therapeutic work.

Research context: High Self-Regulation is the strongest single MAIA-2
predictor of resilience under stress and readiness for trauma-focused
work (Mehling et al., 2018; Van der Kolk, 2014). It indicates that
the nervous system has a route home — a way of returning to a
regulated state from activation. This is a resource that shapes
what is possible in therapeutic work.

# INSTRUMENT 4 — PCL-5
## PTSD Checklist for DSM-5

**What this instrument measures:**
The PCL-5 is the most widely used trauma symptom measure in the world.
It measures 20 symptoms of post-traumatic stress over the past month,
all in relation to a specific stressful experience the person holds in mind.
It covers four clusters of symptoms: re-experiencing (intrusive memories,
nightmares, flashbacks), avoidance (steering clear of reminders), negative
changes in thinking and mood (persistent negative emotions, detachment,
loss of interest), and hyperarousal (being on edge, jumpy, sleep difficulties,
concentration problems). It was developed by the US National Center for PTSD
and is used in clinical and research settings globally.

---

### PCL-MINIMAL — Total score 0 to 10 / Mean descriptor Normal (≤1.23)

Trauma symptoms in this range are minimal or absent. The experience that
was held in mind during this section is not producing significant ongoing
distress across the four symptom clusters. This does not mean the experience
was not significant — it means its current impact on daily functioning
is low.

Research context: Scores in this range fall well within the normative range
for non-clinical populations (Renyer, 2016). The normative mean for adults
without PTSD is approximately 16 — scoring at or below 10 places this
person in the lowest quarter of the non-clinical distribution. This is
a meaningful signal of resilience or recovery.

---

### PCL-MILD — Total score 11 to 20 / Mean descriptor Mild (1.24 to 1.64)

Some trauma symptoms are present but at a mild level. Certain reminders
may still produce distress. There may be moments of heightened alertness
or some tendency to avoid certain people, places, or thoughts associated
with the experience. These symptoms are real and worth acknowledging,
but they are not at a level that typically indicates a full PTSD presentation.

Research context: Scores in this range sit in the lower half of non-clinical
adult distributions (Renyer, 2016). Some ongoing symptom presence is common
after significant stressful experiences — the human nervous system takes time
to process and integrate. Mild scores do not require active treatment but
warrant attention if they are not reducing over time.

---

### PCL-MODERATE — Total score 21 to 30 / Mean descriptor Moderate (1.65 to 2.455)

Trauma symptoms are present at a meaningful level. Re-experiencing,
avoidance, mood changes, or hyperarousal — or some combination —
are affecting daily life with some consistency. This is a level of
trauma symptom burden that warrants clinical attention.

Research context: Scores in this range sit above the non-clinical population
mean of approximately 16 (Renyer, 2016) and represent a clinically notable
elevation. While below the threshold typically used for probable PTSD (33),
this level of symptom burden is associated with meaningful functional
impairment and is worth addressing proactively.

---

### PCL-MODERATELY-SEVERE — Total score 31 to 40 / Mean descriptor Moderately Severe (2.456 to 3.265)

Trauma symptoms are significant. The experience being held in mind is
producing ongoing distress across multiple dimensions. Re-experiencing,
avoidance, emotional changes, and physical hyperarousal may all be
present with some frequency and intensity. Daily functioning is affected.

Research context: Scores at or above 33 are used clinically to indicate
probable PTSD — the point at which trauma symptoms are severe enough
to be considered clinically significant by the VA and in published
research (Bovin et al., 2016). Scores in this range place this person
above approximately 83% of the non-clinical adult population and in
the lower half of clinical PTSD populations. Trauma-focused clinical
support is indicated.

---

### PCL-SEVERE — Total score 41 and above / Mean descriptor Severe to Extremely Severe (above 3.265)

Trauma symptoms are severe. The impact of the experience being held
in mind is pervasive — affecting mood, relationships, the ability to
feel safe, sleep, concentration, and the capacity to engage with life.
This is a significant trauma load that requires professional clinical support.

Research context: Scores in this range place this person in the upper
range of clinical PTSD populations (Bovin et al., 2016; clinical mean
approximately 37, SD 21). Research shows that PTSD at this severity
level is associated with substantial functional impairment across work,
relationships, and physical health. It also responds well to evidence-based
trauma-focused treatments. High scores are not a ceiling — they are
a starting point.

---

### PCL-CLUSTER-B — Re-experiencing cluster dominant (Cluster B mean highest)

The most prominent trauma symptoms are intrusive ones — the experience
breaking through into present consciousness in ways that were not chosen.
Memories that return uninvited, dreams connected to what happened,
moments of feeling as though it is happening again, distress or physical
reactions when something acts as a reminder. The past is not staying
in the past.

Research context: Dominant intrusion symptoms indicate active processing —
the nervous system is attempting to integrate material it has not yet
been able to file away (Ehlers & Clark, 2000). This is often more
responsive to trauma-processing approaches such as EMDR, prolonged
exposure, and CPT than avoidance-dominant profiles, because the material
is already surfacing. The challenge is doing that processing with enough
support and pacing that it does not overwhelm.

---

### PCL-CLUSTER-C — Avoidance cluster dominant (Cluster C mean highest)

The most prominent trauma symptoms are avoidant ones — organised effort
to stay away from reminders of what happened. This might mean avoiding
certain people, places, or situations. It might mean working to keep
certain thoughts or feelings at bay. Avoidance is one of the most
effective short-term coping strategies the nervous system has. It is
also one of the most costly long-term, because it prevents the natural
processing that would otherwise occur.

Research context: Dominant avoidance is associated with longer PTSD
duration and, without intervention, maintenance of the trauma response
over time (Foa & Kozak, 1986). The very effectiveness of avoidance
at reducing immediate distress reinforces its use, creating a pattern
that becomes harder to exit over time. The cost is significant — the
world gradually shrinks to accommodate the avoidance.

---

### PCL-CLUSTER-D — Negative cognitions and mood cluster dominant (Cluster D mean highest)

The most prominent trauma symptoms involve changes in thinking and
emotional experience — persistent negative emotions, a changed sense
of self or the world, difficulty experiencing positive feelings,
feeling detached from others or from life itself. What happened has
not only produced symptoms. It has altered the inner narrative —
the story told about who this person is, what they deserve, and
whether the world is safe.

Research context: Dominant Cluster D symptoms are closely associated
with co-occurring depression and with the formation of trauma-related
core beliefs (Ehlers & Clark, 2000). The negative cognitions are
not simply thoughts — they are conclusions the nervous system drew
from the experience in order to make sense of it. They are also
among the most directly addressable aspects of trauma through
cognitive processing approaches.

---

### PCL-CLUSTER-E — Hyperarousal cluster dominant (Cluster E mean highest)

The most prominent trauma symptoms involve the nervous system being
in a state of sustained alertness — irritability, being easily startled,
feeling on guard, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance.
The body has learned that threat is always possible and has organised
itself accordingly. This is a physiological state, not a psychological
weakness. The nervous system is doing what it learned to do.

Research context: Dominant hyperarousal is associated with sympathetic
nervous system dysregulation and is a strong indicator that somatic
and polyvagal-informed approaches are indicated (Van der Kolk, 2014;
Porges, 2011). The nervous system cannot be talked out of hyperarousal —
it needs to experience safety at a physiological level. This cluster
also has the most direct overlap with sleep, concentration, and
daily functioning impairment.

---

### PCL-ALGORITHM-MET — DSM-5 symptom pattern met across all four clusters

The specific pattern of symptoms required for a provisional DSM-5
PTSD presentation is present — at least one intrusion symptom,
at least one avoidance symptom, at least two cognitive and mood
symptoms, and at least two hyperarousal symptoms, all at a moderate
level or above. This is not a diagnosis. This is a screening
assessment. But the pattern is there.

Research context: The DSM-5 diagnostic algorithm for PTSD was developed
to identify the specific symptom constellation that research has associated
with significant functional impairment and that responds to trauma-focused
treatment (Weathers et al., 2013). Meeting the algorithm is clinically
meaningful independent of the total score — the pattern tells a story
that the number alone does not.

---

# INSTRUMENT 5 — PID-5-SF
## Personality Inventory for DSM-5 — Short Form

**What this instrument measures:**
The PID-5-SF measures personality traits — the enduring patterns in how
a person tends to experience emotions, relate to others, and engage with
the world. These are not symptoms that appeared recently. They are the
longer-standing landscape of personality that shapes how a person moves
through life. The instrument measures traits that exist on a continuum —
everyone has some level of every trait measured here. What the scores
indicate is whether certain traits are present at a level that creates
significant difficulty. Higher scores indicate greater presence of that
trait. They are not labels. They are dimensions.

---

## NEGATIVE AFFECTIVITY DOMAIN

**What it measures:**
How intensely and frequently negative emotions are experienced — anxiety,
emotional instability, fears of abandonment, difficulty regulating
emotional responses. This domain captures the emotional temperature
of daily life.

### NA-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Negative emotional experiences are within the typical range. Difficult
emotions arise — as they do for everyone — but they do not dominate
the emotional landscape or significantly interfere with daily functioning.

Research context: Below the 84th percentile places this person in the
typical range for this personality domain in the general adult population
(Miller et al., 2022). Some negative affect is universal. This level
does not represent a clinical concern.

### NA-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Negative emotions are a more prominent feature of daily experience than
for most people. There is a tendency to feel things intensely, to worry,
or to find emotional regulation effortful. This is a meaningful elevation
but not yet at the most severe level.

Research context: Scores in the elevated range (above the 84th percentile,
one standard deviation above the population mean) represent a notably
higher level of negative affectivity than most people experience
(Miller et al., 2022). Research links elevated negative affectivity
to increased risk of anxiety disorders, depression, and interpersonal
difficulties. It is also associated with greater sensitivity to stress
and a lower threshold for emotional activation.

### NA-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Intense, frequent, and diverse negative emotions are a central feature
of daily life. Emotional experiences feel more powerful and harder to
manage than for most people. This is not a character flaw. It is a
pattern that has likely developed for reasons — often rooted in early
experience, relational history, and what the nervous system has learned
about the world.

Research context: Scores above the 93rd percentile represent clinically
significant elevation in negative affectivity (Miller et al., 2022).
This level is associated with significant difficulty regulating emotional
responses, greater vulnerability to mood and anxiety disorders, and
a tendency to experience even ordinary life demands as emotionally
demanding. It is also associated with patterns of attachment insecurity
and interpersonal sensitivity.

---

## DETACHMENT DOMAIN

**What it measures:**
The degree of withdrawal from social and emotional experience —
the tendency to pull back from connection, to experience reduced
pleasure, or to maintain emotional distance from others and from life.

### DET-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Engagement with others and with life's pleasures is within the typical
range. There is not a strong pull toward withdrawal or emotional distance.

Research context: Below the 84th percentile places this person in the
typical range for Detachment (Miller et al., 2022). Some preference
for solitude and some variation in social engagement is normal.
This level does not represent a clinical concern.

### DET-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

There is a meaningful tendency toward withdrawal — from social interaction,
from emotional engagement, or from the pleasures of daily life. This is
more pronounced than for most people but has not reached the most
significant level.

Research context: Elevated Detachment is associated with social withdrawal,
reduced hedonic capacity, and interpersonal distance (Miller et al., 2022;
Krueger et al., 2012). It is commonly seen in depression and in trauma
presentations where withdrawal has become a protective strategy.
The distinction between chosen solitude and protective withdrawal
matters clinically and is worth exploring.

### DET-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Withdrawal from connection, pleasure, and emotional engagement is a
significant feature of daily experience. The world may feel distant,
flat, or not particularly worth engaging with. Relationships may feel
effortful or beside the point. This is one of the patterns that most
affects quality of life and is worth understanding in its origins
as well as its current expression.

Research context: Clinically significant Detachment is associated with
anhedonia, social isolation, and in more severe presentations with
depressive and schizoid-spectrum features (Miller et al., 2022).
Research consistently links high Detachment with complex trauma —
particularly where withdrawal became an adaptive response to relational
danger. Understanding what the withdrawal was protecting against
is often the beginning of being able to step out of it.

---

## DISINHIBITION DOMAIN

**What it measures:**
The tendency toward impulsive, unplanned action — acting on immediate
impulse without considering consequences, difficulty following through
on commitments, distraction from goals.

### DIS-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Behavioural regulation is within the typical range. There is reasonable
capacity to pause before acting, to follow through on commitments, and
to maintain focus on what matters.

Research context: Below the 84th percentile is the typical range for
Disinhibition (Miller et al., 2022). Some impulsivity is universal.
This level does not represent a clinical concern.

### DIS-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

There is a meaningful tendency toward impulsive action — acting before
fully thinking through consequences, finding it harder to pause when
activated, or struggling to follow through consistently on commitments.

Research context: Elevated Disinhibition is associated with emotional
dysregulation, ADHD-adjacent patterns, and trauma-related nervous system
dysregulation (Miller et al., 2022). The impulsivity here is often
not deliberate — it is the nervous system moving from activation
to action without an adequate regulatory step in between.

### DIS-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Acting on impulse is a consistent and significant pattern. Decisions
made in moments of activation are often regretted. Commitments are
harder to maintain. The gap between feeling something and acting
on it is narrow. This pattern often carries significant shame, because
the person can usually see in retrospect what they wish they had
done differently.

Research context: Clinically significant Disinhibition is associated
with substantial interpersonal and functional consequences (Miller et al.,
2022). Research links it to emotional dysregulation, trauma sequelae,
and patterns where impulsive action has functioned as a way to manage
unbearable affect. Understanding the emotion that preceded the action
is often more useful than addressing the action itself.

---

## EMOTIONAL LABILITY facet

**What it measures:**
How stable or volatile emotional experience is — whether emotions tend
to shift rapidly, arise intensely, or feel disproportionate to the
situation.

### ELABILITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Emotional experience is relatively stable. Moods do not shift rapidly
or intensely without reason. This is within the typical range.

### ELABILITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Emotions tend to be more intense and more changeable than for most people.
Small things may produce stronger reactions. Moods can shift without a
clear external cause. This is noticeable and effortful to manage.

Research context: Elevated Emotional Lability is associated with
borderline-type emotional patterns and with nervous system dysregulation
following trauma or chronic stress (Miller et al., 2022). The emotional
intensity is real — not manufactured or exaggerated. It reflects a
nervous system that has learned to respond quickly and strongly.

### ELABILITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Emotional experience is highly volatile. Moods change rapidly and
intensely, often feeling disproportionate to circumstances. The emotional
state can feel unpredictable even from the inside — it is hard to know
where the feelings will go from moment to moment. This is exhausting
to live with and often deeply confusing.

Research context: Clinically significant Emotional Lability at this
level is strongly associated with difficulty maintaining relational
stability and with patterns of impulsive response to emotional activation
(Miller et al., 2022; Linehan, 1993). The volatility reflects a nervous
system in which the regulatory brake has insufficient power against the
emotional accelerator. This is not a permanent state — it is a pattern
that responds to appropriate support and skill building.

---

## ANXIOUSNESS facet

**What it measures:**
The degree to which anxiety, worry, and anticipation of threat are
persistent features of experience — not as occasional responses to
real danger but as a general orientation toward the world.

### ANXIOUSNESS-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Anxiety is within the typical range. Worry is occasional rather than
pervasive. The world does not feel primarily threatening.

### ANXIOUSNESS-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Anxiety and worry are more present than for most people. There is a
tendency to anticipate problems, to scan for what might go wrong,
to carry a background level of nervous tension that does not fully
lift. This is a meaningful elevation that affects how the world feels.

Research context: Elevated Anxiousness at the trait level is associated
with generalised anxiety patterns and with a nervous system calibrated
toward threat detection (Miller et al., 2022). Unlike state anxiety
that comes and goes, trait anxiousness is a persistent orientation —
the baseline from which everything else is experienced.

### ANXIOUSNESS-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Anxiety is a pervasive and persistent feature of experience. Worry,
tension, anticipation of threat, and fearfulness are not occasional
visitors — they are consistent companions. The nervous system is
oriented toward what might go wrong, what is dangerous, and what
needs to be prepared for.

Research context: Clinically significant Anxiousness at this level
is associated with significant impairment in daily functioning,
sleep difficulties, and physical tension symptoms (Miller et al., 2022).
Research links it to sustained sympathetic nervous system activation —
the body in a low-grade alert state. It is also closely connected
to the flight trauma response — a nervous system that has learned
to stay ready.

---

## SEPARATION INSECURITY facet

**What it measures:**
Fear of rejection, abandonment, or being without close connection.
How much the prospect of being alone or losing significant relationships
generates anxiety and desperate efforts to prevent it.

### SEP-INSECURITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Concerns about rejection or abandonment are within the typical range.
Relationships are important but their possible loss does not generate
overwhelming fear.

### SEP-INSECURITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Fear of rejection and abandonment is more prominent than for most people.
The possibility of being left, rejected, or without close connection
generates significant anxiety. Relationships may be experienced as
fragile — requiring active effort to maintain.

Research context: Elevated Separation Insecurity is associated with
anxious attachment patterns and with relational hypersensitivity
(Miller et al., 2022; Mikulincer & Shaver, 2007). The fear is real
and has typically been learned — from early experiences in which
connection was uncertain or loss was experienced.

### SEP-INSECURITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Fear of abandonment and rejection is intense and pervasive. The prospect
of being without significant connection — or of losing someone important —
generates overwhelming anxiety. This can drive intense efforts to maintain
relationships, sometimes at significant personal cost.

Research context: Clinically significant Separation Insecurity at this
level is associated with disorganised or anxious attachment patterns,
with relational instability, and with a history in which connection
was experienced as unpredictable or unsafe (Miller et al., 2022;
Main & Hesse, 1990). The intensity of the fear reflects how much
connection has mattered — and how much its loss has cost.

---

## WITHDRAWAL facet

**What it measures:**
Preference for being alone over being with others — reduced interest
in social contact, avoidance of social situations, reticence in
interpersonal settings.

### WITHDRAWAL-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Social engagement is within the typical range. Some preference for
solitude is normal and present, but connection with others is also
sought and valued.

### WITHDRAWAL-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

There is a meaningful preference for being alone, more than most people.
Social situations may feel tiring or aversive. The pull toward solitude
is stronger than toward connection.

Research context: Elevated Withdrawal is associated with both genuine
preference for solitude (schizoid presentation) and with trauma-driven
isolation where withdrawal has become protective (Miller et al., 2022).
The distinction matters clinically. In trauma presentations, withdrawal
often reflects learned safety — the world proved that connection meant
danger, so distance became the solution.

### WITHDRAWAL-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Active preference for being alone, with little interest in social
connection or contact with others. Social situations are avoided
or endured. The social world feels largely outside of interest or relevance.

Research context: Clinically significant Withdrawal at this level is
associated with significant social isolation and reduced quality of life
(Miller et al., 2022). Research distinguishes constitutional preference
for solitude from protective withdrawal following relational trauma —
the treatment implication differs substantially between the two.

---

## ANHEDONIA facet

**What it measures:**
Reduced capacity for pleasure — diminished ability to enjoy activities,
relationships, or experiences that might reasonably be expected to
generate positive feeling.

### ANHEDONIA-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

The capacity for pleasure and engagement with life is within the typical
range. Things can be enjoyed. Life has moments of genuine positive
experience.

### ANHEDONIA-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

The capacity for pleasure is meaningfully reduced. Things that used
to feel enjoyable may feel flat. Activities are engaged in out of
habit or obligation rather than genuine interest or anticipated reward.

Research context: Elevated Anhedonia is associated with depressive
presentations and with trauma-related emotional numbing (Miller et al.,
2022). Unlike low mood, anhedonia is specifically about the absence
of positive experience rather than the presence of negative experience.
It often responds to behavioural activation approaches that gently
reintroduce experiences of reward and engagement.

### ANHEDONIA-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

The capacity for pleasure is severely reduced. Very little generates
genuine positive feeling. Life may feel flat, empty, or pointless from
a hedonic perspective — not necessarily painful, but without warmth
or colour.

Research context: Clinically significant Anhedonia is one of the most
treatment-refractory aspects of depression and is associated with
significant loss of quality of life (Miller et al., 2022). Research
links it to dysregulation of the brain's reward system — a genuine
neurobiological consequence of sustained depression or trauma.
It is also one of the patterns that most directly affects motivation
for change, because the anticipated reward of things being different
is itself muted.

---

## INTIMACY AVOIDANCE facet

**What it measures:**
Avoidance of close, intimate, or romantic relationships — discomfort
with emotional closeness, preference for emotional distance even within
relationships.

### INTIMACY-AVOID-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Closeness in relationships is sought or at least tolerated within
the typical range. Emotional intimacy does not generate significant
discomfort.

### INTIMACY-AVOID-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

There is meaningful discomfort with emotional closeness. Intimate
relationships may be avoided, held at arm's length, or exited when
they begin to feel too close. The pull toward distance is stronger
than the pull toward closeness.

Research context: Elevated Intimacy Avoidance is associated with
dismissive attachment patterns and with relational histories in which
closeness proved unsafe or painful (Miller et al., 2022). The avoidance
is not indifference — it is often a highly organised protection against
a form of connection that has previously caused harm.

### INTIMACY-AVOID-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Close emotional relationships are actively avoided. The prospect of
genuine intimacy generates significant discomfort, and the preferred
solution is to maintain distance — sometimes within relationships
as well as outside of them.

Research context: Clinically significant Intimacy Avoidance is associated
with dismissive-avoidant attachment, with histories of relational trauma,
and with a nervous system that has learned that emotional closeness
means danger (Miller et al., 2022; Main & Goldwyn, 1984). The distance
is a solution. Understanding what it was a solution to is the therapeutic question.

---

## IMPULSIVITY facet

**What it measures:**
Acting on urges in the moment without planning or consideration of
consequences — the gap between feeling and acting.

### IMPULSIVITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Impulse regulation is within the typical range. There is generally
a pause between feeling something and acting on it.

### IMPULSIVITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Acting on impulse is more common than for most people. The pause
between feeling and acting is shorter than typical, and actions
are sometimes regretted when considered afterwards.

Research context: Elevated Impulsivity is associated with emotional
dysregulation and with trauma sequelae where the nervous system
has learned to move quickly from activation to action (Miller et al.,
2022). It often co-occurs with Emotional Lability — the intensity
of the emotion drives the speed of the action.

### IMPULSIVITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Impulsive action is a consistent and significant pattern. The moment
of feeling overwhelms the moment of pausing. Decisions are made quickly,
under emotional pressure, and are often regretted. The pattern is
usually well known to the person themselves — the challenge is not
insight but the availability of the regulatory step that slowing down requires.

Research context: Clinically significant Impulsivity is associated with
relational ruptures, self-sabotaging behaviours, and significant
functional consequences (Miller et al., 2022; Linehan, 1993).
Research frames it as the behavioural expression of emotional
dysregulation — the action is an attempt to manage unbearable affect
when no internal regulatory resource is available.

---

## IRRESPONSIBILITY facet

**What it measures:**
Difficulty meeting obligations, following through on commitments,
and maintaining reliability in daily life.

### IRRESPONSIBILITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Follow-through on commitments and daily obligations is within
the typical range.

### IRRESPONSIBILITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Maintaining obligations and commitments is meaningfully more difficult
than for most people. Promises are harder to keep. Tasks are left
incomplete. This is not about motivation or values — it is often
about executive functioning and the capacity to move from intention
to action.

Research context: Elevated Irresponsibility is associated with
executive function difficulties, depressive avoidance, and
trauma-related cognitive dysregulation (Miller et al., 2022).
The pattern often looks like carelessness from the outside
while feeling like overwhelm from the inside.

### IRRESPONSIBILITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Following through on obligations and commitments is a consistent
challenge. The gap between what is intended and what is done is
significant and affects multiple areas of life.

Research context: Clinically significant Irresponsibility is associated
with substantial consequences in occupational and relational functioning
(Miller et al., 2022). Research links it to ADHD-adjacent patterns,
depressive avoidance, and nervous system dysregulation. Understanding
what underlies the failure to follow through is more clinically useful
than addressing the behaviour directly.

---

## DISTRACTIBILITY facet

**What it measures:**
Difficulty sustaining focus and completing tasks due to attention
being easily captured by competing stimuli.

### DISTRACTIBILITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Sustained attention and task completion are within the typical range.

### DISTRACTIBILITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Maintaining focus is more difficult than for most people. Attention
is easily captured by other things. Tasks take longer or are left
incomplete because of difficulty sustaining focus.

Research context: Elevated Distractibility is associated with
ADHD-adjacent patterns, anxiety, and trauma-related hyperarousal
(Miller et al., 2022). The nervous system in a hyperaroused state
attends to the environment broadly — which evolved as a survival
advantage but becomes costly in contexts requiring sustained focus.

### DISTRACTIBILITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Sustained attention is a significant and consistent challenge.
Completing tasks is difficult. The mind moves quickly between things,
and the effort required to hold focus on one thing is substantially
greater than for most people.

Research context: Clinically significant Distractibility at this level
is associated with significant functional impairment across work,
study, and daily tasks (Miller et al., 2022). Research links it
to hyperarousal, anxiety, and attentional dysregulation that may
have origins in chronic stress or trauma.

---

## DEPRESSIVITY facet

**What it measures:**
Persistent feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, and misery
at a trait level — not just in the past two weeks but as a
general feature of how life and self are experienced.

### DEPRESSIVITY-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Trait-level depressive thinking is within the typical range. Low mood
occurs but is not a persistent or defining feature of self-experience.

### DEPRESSIVITY-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

A persistent depressive quality to self-experience is present —
feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, or misery that are not
just recent but have a longer-standing character. This is more
than a rough patch. It is a pattern.

Research context: Elevated Depressivity at the trait level is associated
with dysthymic presentations and with a core belief system that has
been shaped by depressive experience (Miller et al., 2022).
Unlike the PHQ-8 which measures the past two weeks, this facet
measures how things generally are. Elevation here alongside
PHQ-8 elevation suggests both a current episode and a persistent
depressive base.

### DEPRESSIVITY-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, and misery are a persistent
and significant feature of self-experience. This is not just how
things have been recently. It reflects a longer-standing way of
experiencing the self and the future. The inner narrative carries
the weight of this consistently.

Research context: Clinically significant Depressivity at this level
is one of the most important findings in the PID-5-SF for treatment
planning (Miller et al., 2022). Research consistently shows it predicts
poorer treatment response to brief interventions and indicates the
need for sustained clinical work. It also captures the cognitive
and identity-level impact of depression that goes beyond symptom
management — the story the person tells about who they are.

---

## UNUSUAL BELIEFS AND EXPERIENCES facet

**What it measures:**
The degree to which unconventional, magical, or perceptually unusual
beliefs and experiences are present — things that feel real or true
but that most people would not share or relate to.

### UNUSUAL-BELIEFS-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Beliefs and perceptual experiences are within the conventional range.

### UNUSUAL-BELIEFS-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Some unconventional beliefs or unusual experiences are present —
things that feel meaningful or real but that are outside most people's
frame of reference. Context matters enormously here: spiritual,
cultural, and intuitive frameworks can produce elevated scores
without clinical significance.

Research context: Elevated Unusual Beliefs requires careful contextual
interpretation (Miller et al., 2022). In many cultural and spiritual
contexts, elevated scores reflect a meaningful framework rather than
a clinical concern. In trauma contexts, unusual experiences may
reflect altered states associated with hyperarousal or dissociation.
Clinical judgment about context is essential before drawing conclusions.

### UNUSUAL-BELIEFS-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Unconventional beliefs or unusual perceptual experiences are a
significant feature. These feel genuinely real and meaningful
to the person even when they fall outside shared consensus reality.

Research context: Clinically significant elevation requires careful
clinical exploration (Miller et al., 2022). The clinical meaning
depends heavily on context — cultural background, spiritual framework,
trauma history, and the person's own relationship to these experiences.
Scores at this level warrant thoughtful inquiry rather than immediate
pathologising.

---

## PERCEPTUAL DYSREGULATION facet

**What it measures:**
The presence of dissociative, derealization, or depersonalization
experiences — moments where the sense of reality, self, or body
feels altered, distant, or unreal. This is the primary dissociation
indicator in this assessment.

### PERCEPTUAL-DYSREG-LOWER — Below 84th percentile

Dissociative and perceptual disruption experiences are minimal or absent.
The sense of self, body, and reality is generally stable and continuous.

### PERCEPTUAL-DYSREG-ELEVATED — 84th to 93rd percentile

Some experiences of perceptual disruption are present — moments
of feeling detached from oneself or one's surroundings, of things
feeling unreal, of losing track of time or of feeling like an
observer of one's own life. These experiences are more common
than for most people and are worth understanding.

Research context: Elevated Perceptual Dysregulation is associated
with trauma history, particularly complex or relational trauma
(Miller et al., 2022). Research links dissociative experiences
to the nervous system's use of perceptual alteration as a
protective strategy when direct experience of threat is
too overwhelming (Lanius et al., 2010). These experiences
are real — not imagined or invented — and reflect how the
nervous system managed something that was too much to stay
fully present with.

### PERCEPTUAL-DYSREG-SIGNIFICANT — Above 93rd percentile

Significant dissociative and perceptual disruption is present.
Experiences of derealization (the world feeling unreal), depersonalization
(feeling detached from oneself), losing time, or feeling controlled
by forces outside oneself are consistent and clinically significant.
This is the most important pacing indicator in this assessment.

Research context: Clinically significant Perceptual Dysregulation
is associated with complex trauma history and is a key indicator
that trauma-focused work requires careful pacing and stabilisation
before processing begins (Miller et al., 2022; Lanius et al., 2010).
Research is clear that dissociative presentations require modified
treatment approaches where safety and grounding precede any
engagement with trauma material. These experiences are not a
sign of instability — they are a sign of how much the nervous
system has had to manage, and how hard it has worked to protect.

# CROSS-INSTRUMENT SYNTHESIS SECTION

**What this section adds:**
The five instruments above each tell their own story. This section
draws the threads together — looking across all five to understand
how the patterns interact, what they illuminate about each other,
and what the overall picture suggests.

This synthesis is a set of hypotheses drawn from validated instrument
scores. It is a starting point for clinical contact, not a conclusion
about the person. The person in front of you will always be more
than any instrument can capture.

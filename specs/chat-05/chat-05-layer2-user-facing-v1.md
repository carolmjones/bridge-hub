# INSTRUMENT 1 — PSS-10

**What this instrument measures:**
The PSS-10 looks at how often, over the past month, life has felt
uncontrollable, unpredictable, or overwhelming. It measures the
subjective experience of stress, not the number of stressful events,
but how much pressure you have felt from your circumstances. Two people
can face identical situations and experience them very differently.
The PSS-10 measures that difference.

---

### PSS-LOW — Score 0 to 13

In this range, stress does not appear to be experienced as overwhelming.
Life feels broadly manageable, and there is a general sense of being
able to cope with what is being asked. This does not mean life is without
difficulty. It means the demands appear proportionate to the capacity
to meet them.

The average PSS-10 score in the general adult population is around 15 to
16 (Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2012). A score in this range places you below
that average, suggesting a stress load that is lighter than most people
experience day to day. This is a meaningful resource, a sense of
manageability that not everyone has access to.

---

### PSS-MODERATE — Score 14 to 26

In this range, stress appears to be a real and present feature of life.
There may be moments of feeling overloaded, of struggling to keep up,
of sensing that circumstances are not fully within control. This is the
most common experience. The majority of adults score in this range.
Being here is not a sign that something is wrong. It is a sign that life
is making real demands.

Most adults in the general population score between 14 and 26 on the
PSS-10. This range has been associated with some difficulty managing
competing demands and periodic feelings of loss of control, but also
with retained capacity to cope (Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2012). Whether
the stress feels more like helplessness or more like self-doubt carries
different meaning and is explored further below where relevant.

---

### PSS-HIGH — Score 27 to 40

In this range, stress appears to have become something closer to a
constant presence. Life may feel unpredictable, difficult to control,
and at times overwhelming. The sense that difficulties are piling up
faster than they can be managed may feel familiar. This is not a
character flaw or a sign of weakness. It is a physiological and
psychological response to sustained pressure that has exceeded the
system's current capacity to absorb it.

Sustained stress in this range has been associated with elevated cortisol
levels, suppressed immune function, increased susceptibility to infection,
slower wound healing, and markers of accelerated biological aging
(Cohen & Janicki-Deverts, 2012). The body and the mind do not separate
stress from its physical toll. What you are carrying is real, significant,
and worth taking seriously.

---

### PSS-HELPLESSNESS-HIGH — Helplessness subscale elevated

This pattern suggests that stress is showing up primarily as a sense that
life is happening to you rather than being shaped by you. Circumstances
feel impossible to influence. Things feel out of your hands. This is one
of the most exhausting forms of stress, because there is no clear action
that feels available.

Perceived helplessness is closely linked to what researchers call learned
helplessness, a state in which the nervous system stops expecting that
effort will produce change and begins conserving energy by withdrawing
from attempts to influence outcomes (Seligman, 1975). This pattern is
associated with increased risk of depression and burnout when it persists
over time (Cohen & Williamson, 1988).

---

### PSS-EFFICACY-LOW — Self-efficacy subscale low

This pattern suggests that stress is showing up primarily as doubt in
your own capacity to handle what life brings. Even manageable demands
may feel threatening, not because life is necessarily out of control,
but because the sense that you can meet it appears to be absent right now.

Self-efficacy is one of the most robust predictors of resilience under
stress. When it is intact, people tolerate higher stress loads without
the same physiological consequences. When it collapses, even moderate
stress becomes more damaging (Bandura, 1997; Cohen & Williamson, 1988).
Rebuilding it through small, concrete experiences of managing and
succeeding is often the most direct route through this pattern.

# INSTRUMENT 2 — PHQ-8

**What this instrument measures:**
The PHQ-8 looks at the presence and severity of depressive symptoms over
the past two weeks. It covers eight core dimensions: loss of pleasure,
low mood, sleep disturbance, fatigue, appetite changes, feelings of
worthlessness, concentration difficulties, and changes in how the body
moves or feels. It was specifically designed for use in settings where
a clinician is not present to follow up on every response, making it
well suited to this kind of screening.

---

### PHQ-NONE — Score 0 to 4

Depressive symptoms in this range are not at a clinically concerning
level. There may be difficult days and moments of low mood, that is
part of being human, but they do not appear to be forming a pattern
that warrants clinical attention. The majority of people in the general
population score in this range.

Approximately 76% of adults in general population samples score below 5
on the PHQ-8 (Kroenke et al., 2009). A score in this range is consistent
with the absence of a depressive pattern. If low mood is present at all,
it appears situational and responsive rather than persistent and pervasive.

---

### PHQ-MILD — Score 5 to 9

Some depressive symptoms appear to be present. They may not feel
overwhelming, but they are there: a flatness, a difficulty finding
pleasure in things that used to feel meaningful, some tiredness that
does not fully lift. This range is worth paying attention to, not because
it signals crisis, but because it suggests that something in the emotional
system is under strain.

Approximately 18% of adults score in this range in general population
studies (Kroenke et al., 2009). Mild depressive symptoms are associated
with reduced daily functioning and lower quality of life even without
meeting clinical criteria for a depressive pattern. They also represent
a point at which support can prevent progression to a more significant
episode.

---

### PHQ-MODERATE — Score 10 to 14

Depressive symptoms appear to be present and affecting daily life. They
may be consistent enough to interfere with the ability to do what needs
to be done: to concentrate, to sustain interest, to feel adequate, to
maintain energy and sleep. This is not a light load. It is a level of
symptoms that warrants attention and support.

A score of 10 or above on the PHQ has a sensitivity of 81% and a positive
predictive value of 92% for a depressive disorder (Kroenke et al., 2001).
Scoring here suggests there is a meaningful likelihood that clinical
support is indicated. This is not a screening false positive. It is a
signal worth taking seriously.

---

### PHQ-MODERATELY-SEVERE — Score 15 to 19

Depressive symptoms at this level appear to be substantially disrupting
daily life. They may be consistent, pervasive, and affecting the ability
to function across most areas. Getting through the day may require
significant effort. Things that used to feel manageable may feel
impossible right now.

Scores in this range are commonly associated with clinically significant
depressive symptoms and substantial disruption to daily functioning
(Kroenke et al., 2009). This does not diagnose depression, but it does
suggest that professional support would be appropriate. Research
consistently shows that depressive symptoms at this level respond well
to treatment. This is not a fixed state. It is a point in a trajectory
that can change with the right support.

---

### PHQ-SEVERE — Score 20 to 24

Depressive symptoms at this level appear to be profound and pervasive.
They may be present across nearly all dimensions: mood, energy, sleep,
concentration, sense of self-worth. This is one of the heaviest loads
the emotional system can carry, and it warrants prompt professional
attention.

Scores in this range are commonly associated with severe depressive
symptoms that profoundly disrupt daily functioning (Kroenke et al., 2009).
Research indicates that symptoms at this level typically respond best to
combined support, both psychological and, where appropriate, in
consultation with a prescriber. This is not a sign that things cannot
get better. It is a sign of how much the system is currently carrying
and how much support it needs right now.

---

### PHQ-SOMATIC-HIGH — Physical items elevated (items 3, 4, 5, 8)

The physical dimensions of depressive symptoms appear to be particularly
prominent in your results: sleep, energy, appetite, and how the body
feels in daily movement. Depressive symptoms do not only live in thoughts
and feelings. They live in the body: in the tiredness that sleep does not
fix, in the appetite that shifts without reason, in the heaviness that
makes movement feel effortful.

Depressive symptoms with prominent physical features are associated with
trauma history and nervous system dysregulation (Kirmayer, 2001). This
is also the presentation most likely to go unrecognised, because the
physical experience is so prominent that the emotional dimension may not
be the first thing named. Approaching this pattern through the body's
experience tends to be more effective than starting with emotional
language alone.

---

### PHQ-COGNITIVE-HIGH — Inner experience items elevated (items 1, 2, 6)

The inner experience of depressive symptoms appears particularly
prominent: loss of pleasure, persistent low mood, and feelings of
worthlessness or failure. These dimensions affect how you see yourself
and your world. When these items are elevated, the narrative of who you
are, what you deserve, and what is possible can become deeply coloured
by the weight of how things feel right now.

High scores on these items are associated with longer-standing or more
entrenched depressive presentations and with negative beliefs about the
self (Beck et al., 1979; Kroenke et al., 2009). They are also the items
most responsive to approaches that work with meaning and inner narrative.
The inner story is both the place of difficulty and the place where
change begins.

# INSTRUMENT 3 — MAIA-2

**What this instrument measures:**
The MAIA-2 looks at your relationship with your body's internal signals.
It covers five dimensions of how you notice, respond to, and use body
sensations in daily life. This is not about physical fitness or health.
It is about whether your body feels like a source of information, a
source of threat, a place of safety, or something largely absent from
conscious experience. These patterns are deeply shaped by history,
especially by experiences that taught the body whether it was safe to
be present in.

No overall total score is calculated for the MAIA-2. Each subscale tells
its own story. The pattern across subscales is more informative than any
single number.

---

## Not avoiding body sensations

This subscale looks at whether you tend to stay present with sensations
of physical discomfort or pain, or whether you move away from them.
Higher scores mean staying present. Lower scores mean moving away from
body experience through distraction, suppression, or avoidance.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile

There appears to be a strong tendency to move away from uncomfortable
body sensations rather than staying with them. When the body signals
discomfort, the default response may be to redirect attention elsewhere,
to push through, to distract, to occupy the mind with something else.
This is a common and understandable pattern, especially when body
experience has historically been associated with pain, threat, or
overwhelm.

Low scores here are associated with avoidant responses to physical
experience and are common where the body has come to feel like a source
of threat rather than information (Mehling et al., 2018). This is not
a character trait. It is a learned protective response. The body learned
to be avoided. It can learn to be approached again, carefully and
gradually.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile

There appears to be some capacity to stay present with body discomfort,
though this is not yet a consistent strength. Sometimes sensations can
be noticed and stayed with. At other times, particularly when discomfort
is more intense, the impulse to distract or redirect may be stronger.
This middle ground is a workable place to build from.

Most people in the general population fall in this range (Mehling et al.,
2018). The capacity to stay with body sensations develops with practice
and with accumulated safety. Being here suggests the foundation is
present even if it is not yet robust.

### NOT-DISTRACTING-STRONG — Above 75th percentile

There appears to be a meaningful capacity to remain present with physical
discomfort without automatically moving away from it. Sensations can be
noticed and tolerated without the immediate impulse to suppress or
redirect. This is a genuine resource, one that many people do not have,
and one that forms the foundation for body-based therapeutic work.

Higher scores here are associated with more adaptive processing of
physical experience and greater capacity for somatic awareness
(Mehling et al., 2018). This capacity supports engagement with and
response to body-based approaches.

---

## Tolerating strong feelings

This subscale looks at whether body sensations tend to trigger anxiety,
catastrophising, or emotional escalation. Higher scores mean sensations
can be noticed without spiralling. Lower scores mean body sensations
tend to amplify into fear or worry.

### NOT-WORRYING-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile

Physical sensations appear to tend toward triggering anxiety and worry.
When something is noticed in the body, the mind may move quickly toward
concern or distress. The body may feel like a source of threat signals
rather than neutral information. This pattern is often exhausting because
the body is never truly quiet.

Low scores here are specifically linked to anxiety and to difficulty
identifying and labelling emotional states (Desdentado et al., 2022).
Research shows that low scores on this subscale are associated with
depression and emotional dysregulation. The body-anxiety feedback loop
is documented: body sensations trigger anxiety, anxiety amplifies body
sensations, and the cycle sustains itself (Mehling et al., 2018).

### NOT-WORRYING-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile

Body sensations do not consistently trigger anxiety, though they
sometimes may, particularly when discomfort is more intense or stress
is already high. There appears to be some capacity to notice a sensation
without immediately worrying about what it means. This is a workable
foundation.

Most adults in the general population fall in this range (Mehling et al.,
2018). The capacity to notice without catastrophising develops with
practice and with experiences that demonstrate that body sensations are
survivable and informative rather than dangerous.

### NOT-WORRYING-STRONG — Above 75th percentile

Body sensations generally appear to be tolerated without triggering
significant anxiety or worry. When something is noticed physically,
it can be observed without the mind moving immediately toward alarm.
This is a meaningful resource, particularly where other parts of this
map suggest stress or emotional intensity.

Higher scores here are associated with lower anxiety reactivity to body
experience and greater emotional stability (Mehling et al., 2018).
This capacity supports the ability to use the body as a source of
information rather than a source of threat.

---

## Seeing yourself clearly

This subscale looks at whether you can deliberately direct and sustain
attention toward your own body experience. Higher scores mean the
capacity to tune inward on purpose. Lower scores mean this kind of
deliberate internal focus is difficult to access or maintain.

### ATTENTION-REGULATION-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile

Deliberately directing attention toward body experience appears
difficult. Attempts to focus on internal body sensations may be brief
or easily disrupted. When attention does turn inward, it may be hard
to hold it there.

Low scores here are associated with difficulty using body-based coping
strategies and with a tendency to think rather than sense (Mehling et al.,
2018). This is often connected to a history of it not feeling safe to
pay close attention to the body. The capacity to direct attention inward
can be developed, usually through gradual, safe practice.

### ATTENTION-REGULATION-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile

Some capacity to direct and sustain attention toward body experience
appears to exist, though it may vary. In quieter moments, attention can
be brought inward. In busier or more distressing moments, this may become
harder to maintain.

Most adults in the general population fall in this range (Mehling et al.,
2018). The ability to attend deliberately to body experience strengthens
with practice. Being here means the foundational capacity is present.

### ATTENTION-REGULATION-STRONG — Above 75th percentile

There appears to be a meaningful capacity to deliberately direct
attention toward body experience and sustain it. This ability to tune
inward on purpose is one of the foundational skills for body-based
regulation and self-awareness.

Higher scores here are associated with greater interoceptive skill and
better outcomes in approaches that draw on body awareness (Mehling et al.,
2018). This is a genuine resource worth building on.

---

## Emotional awareness

This subscale looks at whether you notice and track the body sensations
that accompany emotional experiences. Higher scores mean emotions are
felt as body events. Lower scores mean emotions may be understood as
concepts without being fully experienced physically.

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile

The connection between body sensations and emotional states may not be
well established. Emotions may be understood and named without being
fully felt as physical experience. This is sometimes described as knowing
what you feel without feeling it in the body.

Low scores here are associated with difficulty identifying emotions
through bodily signals, which is particularly common where chronic stress
or dissociation has been present (Mehling et al., 2018; Desdentado et al.,
2022). This is not an absence of emotion. It is a disconnection between
the emotional experience and its physical expression that can, with
support, be gently bridged.

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile

There appears to be some awareness of how emotions manifest in the body,
though this is not yet consistent. At times, emotions can be felt as
physical events: a tightening, a heaviness, a lightness. At other times,
the connection may be less clear.

Most adults in the general population fall in this range (Mehling et al.,
2018). The capacity to feel emotions in the body strengthens with safety
and with gentle, supported practice in paying attention to physical
experience.

### EMOTIONAL-AWARENESS-STRONG — Above 75th percentile

Emotions appear to be experienced as body events as well as mental or
cognitive experiences. There is a meaningful connection between what is
felt emotionally and what is noticed physically. This integration is one
of the foundations of emotional self-awareness and regulation.

Higher scores here are associated with greater emotional clarity and more
nuanced self-awareness (Mehling et al., 2018). This is a significant
resource, particularly where other parts of this map suggest emotional
intensity or difficulty.

---

## Self-regulation

This subscale looks at whether you can use awareness of your own body to
settle, regulate, or resource yourself during distress. Higher scores
mean the body is available as a tool for calming and stabilising. Lower
scores mean this regulatory pathway is not yet accessible.

### SELF-REGULATION-LIMITED — Below 25th percentile

The body may not currently be available as a settling tool. When things
become overwhelming, turning attention toward the body or breath may not
produce a sense of calm. This does not mean regulation is impossible.
It means this particular route may currently be less accessible.

Low self-regulation stands out because it may affect how easily body-based
strategies can be used during support. If turning toward the body increases
distress, the first step is usually not deeper body awareness, but more
safety. Body-based regulatory capacity can be an important foundation for
approaching difficult material safely, especially when body awareness
itself feels activating (Mehling et al., 2018; Van der Kolk, 2014).
This is a sequencing consideration, not a ceiling.

### SELF-REGULATION-DEVELOPING — 25th to 75th percentile

Some capacity to use body awareness for settling appears to exist.
At times, attending to the breath or bringing awareness to physical
sensation may produce a sense of steadiness. At other times, this may
be harder to access, particularly when distress is high.

Most adults in the general population fall in this range (Mehling et al.,
2018). Body-based regulation is a skill that strengthens with practice
and with accumulated experiences of the body as a safe place to return
to. Being here means the pathway is opening.

### SELF-REGULATION-STRONG — Above 75th percentile

The body appears to be genuinely available as a resource for settling
and regulation. When distress arises, there is capacity to turn inward
and find a degree of steadiness there. This is one of the most significant
resources a person can bring to any kind of healing or support work.

Higher scores here are associated with greater resilience under stress
and better capacity to engage with body-based therapeutic approaches
(Mehling et al., 2018). This is a foundation worth recognising and
building on.

# INSTRUMENT 4 — PCL-5

**What this instrument measures:**
The PCL-5 looks at whether difficult or distressing experiences from the
past are still showing up in your daily life. It covers four areas:
unwanted re-experiencing of what happened, avoidance of reminders,
changes in how you think and feel, and changes in how alert or reactive
your body feels. It does not ask you to describe what happened in detail.
It simply asks how much impact it may still be having.

---

### PCL-MINIMAL — Score 0 to 10

The experience you held in mind during this section does not appear to
be producing significant ongoing symptoms right now. This does not mean
it was not significant. It means its current impact on daily functioning
appears to be low.

A score in this range sits within the lower end of the non-clinical
normative distribution (Renyer, 2016). Some residual sensitivity to
reminders is normal after difficult experiences. What this range suggests
is that the experience is not currently dominating your daily life in a
way that warrants clinical attention.

---

### PCL-MILD — Score 11 to 20

Some symptoms appear to be present, but at a mild level. Certain
reminders may still produce moments of distress. There may be some
tendency to avoid certain people, places, or thoughts connected to what
happened. These symptoms are real and worth acknowledging, but they do
not appear to be at a level that significantly disrupts daily functioning.

Scores in this range sit above the non-clinical population average,
suggesting some residual impact from the experience you named
(Renyer, 2016). Mild symptoms at this level can sometimes resolve with
time and support. They can also be an early indicator that some focused
attention would be useful.

---

### PCL-MODERATE — Score 21 to 30

Symptoms appear to be present at a meaningful level. Re-experiencing,
avoidance, mood changes, and heightened alertness may be affecting daily
life with some consistency. The experience you named appears to still be
taking up space in your system in ways that go beyond occasional moments
of difficulty.

Scores in this range sit above the non-clinical population mean and
represent a clinically notable elevation (Renyer, 2016). While below the
threshold commonly used to indicate probable trauma-related disorder,
this level of symptom burden is associated with meaningful functional
impact. It is worth addressing rather than waiting to see if it resolves
on its own.

---

### PCL-MODERATELY-SEVERE — Score 31 to 40

Symptoms appear to be significant. The experience you named may be
producing ongoing distress across multiple dimensions. Re-experiencing,
avoidance, emotional changes, and physical alertness may all be present
with some frequency and intensity. Daily life may be shaped around
managing or avoiding what is still active in your system.

Scores at or approaching 33 are used in clinical settings as an indicator
that trauma-related symptoms warrant further assessment and support
(Bovin et al., 2016). This level of symptom burden stands out as one of
the stronger patterns in your results. Trauma-focused support is likely
to be appropriate.

---

### PCL-SEVERE — Score 41 and above

Symptoms appear to be pervasive, affecting mood, relationships, the
ability to feel safe, sleep, concentration, and the capacity to engage
with daily life. The experience you named may not feel like something
that happened in the past. It may feel like something that is still
happening, at least in part, inside your system.

Scores in this range sit in the upper portion of both non-clinical and
clinical normative distributions (Bovin et al., 2016). Research shows
that trauma-related symptoms at this severity level respond well to
evidence-based trauma-focused support. High scores are not a ceiling.
They are a starting point.

---

### PCL-INTRUSION-HIGH — Re-experiencing area elevated

The re-experiencing dimension of trauma symptoms appears to be
particularly prominent. Unwanted memories, distressing dreams, or moments
of feeling as if the experience is happening again may be recurring.
Emotional and physical reactions to reminders may be intense. This is
the dimension of trauma that feels most like the past is not staying
in the past.

The re-experiencing symptom area reflects the nervous system's continued
activation around the experience. When this is elevated, the system has
not been able to file the experience away as something that is over.
It remains alert to signals that it might happen again. This is
physiological, not a failure of willpower or resilience (Weathers et al.,
2013).

---

### PCL-AVOIDANCE-HIGH — Avoidance area elevated

The avoidance dimension of trauma symptoms appears to be particularly
prominent. There may be consistent efforts to stay away from memories,
thoughts, or feelings connected to the experience, as well as from
people, places, or situations that serve as reminders. Avoidance often
provides short-term relief but tends to maintain the symptoms over time.

The avoidance symptom area reflects the nervous system's attempt to
manage the cost of re-experiencing by limiting exposure to triggers.
It is a protective strategy that made sense at the time. The challenge
is that avoidance prevents the system from receiving the evidence it
needs that the experience is survivable and over (Weathers et al., 2013).

---

### PCL-MOOD-HIGH — Changes in thinking and mood area elevated

Changes in how you think and feel appear to be a particularly prominent
dimension of your results. This may include persistent negative beliefs
about yourself or the world, persistent negative emotions, a changed
sense of who you are, difficulty experiencing positive feelings, or a
sense of distance from others and from life.

This symptom area reflects the ways that overwhelming experiences can
alter the inner narrative: what seems true about yourself, about other
people, and about whether the world is safe. These are not simply
thoughts. They are conclusions the system drew from the experience in
order to make sense of it. They are also among the most directly
addressable aspects of trauma symptoms with the right support
(Weathers et al., 2013; Ehlers & Clark, 2000).

---

### PCL-AROUSAL-HIGH — Changes in alertness and reactivity area elevated

The physical alertness dimension of trauma symptoms appears to be
particularly prominent. This may include feeling irritable or easily
angered, taking risks, being on guard, feeling jumpy or easily startled,
having difficulty concentrating, or difficulty sleeping. The body may
feel as if it is still braced for something that has not yet passed.

This symptom area reflects sustained activation of the nervous system's
threat-detection system. When this is elevated, the body has not received
the signal that it is safe to come down from alert. This is a
physiological state, not a psychological weakness. The nervous system
is doing what it learned to do. It needs experiences of safety at a
physical level, not just reassurance at a cognitive one
(Van der Kolk, 2014; Porges, 2011).

---

### PCL-ALGORITHM-MET — A DSM-5-aligned symptom pattern is present

*(Internal code: PCL-ALGORITHM-MET — never shown to client)*

**Client-facing title: A DSM-5-aligned symptom pattern is present**

Your responses suggest that symptoms appear to be present across all four
DSM-5-aligned trauma symptom areas: re-experiencing, avoidance, changes
in thinking and mood, and changes in alertness and reactivity.

In this scoring method, symptoms rated at moderate or above are counted
as present. Your answers appear to meet the screening pattern used by
the PCL-5: at least one re-experiencing symptom, at least one avoidance
symptom, at least two changes in thinking and mood, and at least two
changes in alertness and reactivity.

This is not a diagnosis. This screening cannot confirm whether all
diagnostic criteria are met. But the pattern is meaningful. It suggests
that the experience you named may still be affecting several parts of
daily life, not just one.

The total score tells us how much symptom load is present. The pattern
across symptom areas tells us how that load is spread. Together, they
give a clearer picture than either one alone (Weathers et al., 2013).

# INSTRUMENT 5 — PID-5-SF

**What this instrument measures:**
The PID-5-SF looks at broader emotional, relational, and behavioural
patterns. It is not designed to measure only how you felt today or this
week. These patterns may have developed over time and may be shaped by
what life has required of you. Everyone has some level of every trait
measured here. What the scores suggest is whether certain patterns are
present strongly enough to be creating difficulty. Higher scores suggest
a stronger presence of that pattern compared with published norms.
They are not labels. They are dimensions of experience.

---

## Negative emotional reactivity

This area looks at how intensely and frequently negative emotions are
experienced: anxiety, emotional instability, fears of being left or
rejected, difficulty settling emotional responses. It captures the
emotional temperature of daily life.

### NA-LOWER — Typical range (below 84th percentile)

Negative emotional experiences appear to be within a typical range.
Difficult emotions arise, as they do for everyone, but they do not appear
to be dominating daily life or significantly interfering with functioning.

Below the 84th percentile places this pattern in the typical range
compared with the general adult population (Miller et al., 2022). Some
negative affect is universal. This level does not suggest concern in
this area.

### NA-ELEVATED — Meaningfully elevated (84th to 93rd percentile)

Negative emotions appear to be a more prominent feature of daily
experience than for most people. There may be a tendency to feel things
intensely, to worry, or to find emotional settling effortful. This is a
meaningful elevation worth paying attention to.

Scores in this range sit above the 84th percentile, one standard
deviation above the population mean (Miller et al., 2022). Research links
this level of negative emotional reactivity to increased sensitivity to
stress, a lower threshold for emotional activation, and greater
vulnerability to anxiety or low mood. This pattern is worth understanding,
not least because it often has clear origins in what life has asked of
the nervous system.

### NA-SIGNIFICANT — Strongly elevated (above 93rd percentile)

Intense, frequent, and diverse negative emotions appear to be a central
feature of daily life. Emotional experiences may feel more powerful and
harder to manage than for most people. This is not a character flaw.
It is a pattern often shaped by earlier experiences, relational history,
stress load, and what the nervous system has learned about safety.

Scores above the 93rd percentile represent a strongly elevated level of
negative emotional reactivity compared with published norms (Miller et al.,
2022). This level is commonly associated with significant difficulty
settling emotional responses, greater vulnerability to anxiety or low
mood, and heightened sensitivity to relational signals. This pattern
stands out as one of the strongest signals in your results.

---

## Withdrawing from relationships

This area looks at the degree of withdrawal from social and emotional
experience: the tendency to pull back from connection, to experience
reduced pleasure, or to maintain emotional distance from others and from
life itself.

### DET-LOWER — Typical range (below 84th percentile)

Engagement with others and with daily pleasures appears to be within a
typical range. There is not a strong pull toward withdrawal or emotional
distance that warrants attention.

Below the 84th percentile is the typical range for this pattern compared
with the general adult population (Miller et al., 2022). Some preference
for solitude and variation in social engagement is normal. This level
does not suggest concern in this area.

### DET-ELEVATED — Meaningfully elevated (84th to 93rd percentile)

There appears to be a meaningful tendency toward withdrawal, from social
interaction, emotional engagement, or the pleasures of daily life. This
is more pronounced than for most people and is worth understanding in
context.

Elevated scores here are associated with social withdrawal, reduced
capacity for pleasure, and interpersonal distance (Miller et al., 2022;
Krueger et al., 2012). In some people, withdrawal can develop as an
adaptive response to relational stress, disappointment, or repeated
overwhelm. The distinction between chosen solitude and protective
withdrawal is worth exploring.

### DET-SIGNIFICANT — Strongly elevated (above 93rd percentile)

Withdrawal from connection, pleasure, and emotional engagement appears
to be a significant feature of daily experience. The world may feel
distant, flat, or harder to engage with. Relationships may feel
effortful. This is one of the patterns that most affects quality of life
and is worth understanding in its origins as well as its current
expression.

Scores in this strongly elevated range are associated with anhedonia,
social isolation, and patterns that can overlap with depressive and
trauma-related presentations (Miller et al., 2022). In some people,
withdrawal can develop as an adaptive response to relational stress,
disappointment, danger, or repeated overwhelm. Understanding what the
withdrawal may have been protecting against is often where the work
begins.

## Impulse and self-control

This area looks at the tendency toward impulsive, unplanned action:
acting on immediate urges before there has been enough space to consider
consequences, difficulty consistently following through even when the
intention is there, distraction from goals.

### DIS-LOWER — Typical range (below 84th percentile)

Behavioural regulation appears to be within a typical range. There
appears to be a reasonable capacity to pause before acting, to follow
through on commitments, and to maintain focus on what matters.

Below the 84th percentile is the typical range for this pattern
(Miller et al., 2022). Some impulsivity is universal. This level does
not suggest concern in this area.

### DIS-ELEVATED — Meaningfully elevated (84th to 93rd percentile)

There appears to be a meaningful tendency toward acting before there
has been enough space to consider consequences, or struggling to follow
through consistently even when the intention is there. This is more
pronounced than for most people.

Elevated scores here are associated with emotional dysregulation and
nervous system dysregulation (Miller et al., 2022). The impulsivity in
this pattern is often not deliberate. It is the nervous system moving
from activation to action without an adequate settling step in between.
Understanding what is being felt in the moment before the action tends
to be more useful than focusing on the action itself.

### DIS-SIGNIFICANT — Strongly elevated (above 93rd percentile)

Acting on impulse appears to be a consistent and significant pattern.
Decisions made in moments of activation may often be regretted.
Consistently following through, even when the intention is there, may
be difficult. The gap between feeling something and acting on it appears
narrow and hard to manage in the moment.

Scores in this strongly elevated range are associated with substantial
interpersonal and functional consequences (Miller et al., 2022). Research
links this pattern to emotional dysregulation and to situations where
impulsive action has served as a way to manage overwhelming feeling.
Understanding the emotion that preceded the action is often more useful
than addressing the action itself. This pattern stands out as one of the
stronger signals in your results.
